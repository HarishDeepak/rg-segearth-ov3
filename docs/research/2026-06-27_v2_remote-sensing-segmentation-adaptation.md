# **Optimizing Open-Vocabulary Remote Sensing Segmentation: Zero-Shot, Test-Time, and Few-Shot Adaptation Strategies for SegEarth-OV-3**

## **Introduction to the Domain Shift Challenge in Open-Vocabulary Segmentation**

The deployment of open-vocabulary semantic segmentation (OVSS) models in remote sensing introduces profound challenges that are rarely encountered in natural image processing. The SegEarth-OV-3 framework represents a significant advancement in this domain, integrating the Promptable Concept Segmentation (PCS) capabilities of the Segment Anything Model 3 (SAM 3\) with a frozen PE-L+ backbone1. This architecture unifies recognition and segmentation, leveraging dual-head mask fusion—which combines fine-grained instance details from the transformer decoder with global semantic coverage—and presence-guided filtering to suppress false positives1. However, evaluating a model optimized on the ISPRS Potsdam dataset at a 5cm Ground Sample Distance (GSD) on Darmstadt DOP20 imagery at a 20cm GSD introduces a severe, 4× resolution degradation4.  
This resolution shift fundamentally alters the geometric, textural, and semantic representation of target classes. Features that exhibit sharp boundaries and distinct internal textures at 5cm (e.g., roof tiles, road markings, individual vehicle features) collapse into homogeneous, low-contrast pixel blocks at 20cm4. Because vision-language models (VLMs) and vision foundation models (VFMs) rely on these localized visual tokens to align with text embeddings in a shared latent space, the 4× resolution gap degrades the zero-shot matching probability, leading to fragmented masks, boundary dissolution, and severe semantic confusion7.  
Addressing this domain gap without initiating full-parameter gradient updates on the computationally heavy SAM 3 backbone requires a sophisticated, multi-tiered approach. The adaptation strategies must navigate the delicate balance between preserving the expansive zero-shot generalization capabilities learned during SAM's pre-training on 1.1 billion masks, and specializing the inference pipeline to the degraded visual realities of the Darmstadt target domain9. The subsequent analysis provides an exhaustive review of advanced text prompt engineering, resolution-aware domain adaptation, gradient-free test-time adaptation (TTA), and parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) tailored for extreme low-data regimes, specifically bounded by a five-shot tile adaptation limit.

## **Advanced Text Prompt Engineering for Open-Vocabulary Models**

The reliance of the SegEarth-OV-3 architecture on text-driven prompting inherently ties segmentation accuracy to the quality of the language-vision alignment. In standard natural image processing, providing a naive class name such as "car" or "building" is often sufficient due to the robust side-profile features captured in training datasets8. However, in overhead Earth Observation, these naive prompts suffer from profound textual ambiguity. A recent systematic evaluation on the LAE-80C benchmark, an aggregation of diverse aerial datasets, revealed that the primary bottleneck in open-vocabulary aerial detection is not visual localization, but rather semantic confusion8. Vision encoders can successfully isolate discrete objects in the imagery, but the text encoders fail to map these isolated pixel clusters to the correct semantic category due to overlapping taxonomies8.

### **Mitigating Semantic Confusion via LLM-Driven Prompt Expansion**

To overcome the limitations of static synonym lists, the Test-Time Multi-Prompt Adaptation (TMPA) framework provides a blueprint for dynamic prompt generation. The core thesis of TMPA is that background classes and target classes carry shifting semantic meanings depending on the specific geospatial context, necessitating a context-aware text prompt generator15. Rather than manually engineering prompts, the system utilizes a Large Language Model (LLM) equipped with a task-driven system prompt that enforces visual diversity constraints. The LLM is instructed to act as an expert in remote sensing and generate multiple distinct, single-sentence descriptions for each category that highlight different visual aspects such as shape, texture, and shadows as they appear from a nadir perspective15.  
For example, when transitioning from the highly detailed 5cm Potsdam imagery to the blurrier 20cm Darmstadt imagery, a static prompt of "residential building" is suboptimal. The LLM expands this into a diverse ensemble of descriptions: "rectangular impervious structures with flat roofs casting sharp directional shadows in overhead imagery," and "dense clusters of artificial geometry exhibiting uniform texture against surrounding natural vegetation." By aggregating the text embeddings of these diverse, context-rich prompts, the model establishes a much broader and more robust semantic manifold in the joint latent space. This approach effectively resolves the textual ambiguity inherent in naive class names, allowing the frozen vision-language components to achieve state-of-the-art segmentation without any parameter updates15.

### **Hierarchical Prompting and Taxonomic Filtering**

The LAE-80C study demonstrated that reducing the vocabulary size from an overlapping 80-class taxonomy to distinct, non-overlapping parent categories yielded a 15-fold improvement in open-vocabulary performance8. This finding underscores the necessity of hierarchical prompting in zero-shot applications. When processing the Darmstadt dataset, prompting the model simultaneously with fine-grained sub-categories (e.g., "sedan," "pickup truck," "delivery van") introduces massive semantic interference, as these vehicles exhibit near-identical visual signatures at a 20cm GSD6.  
An optimal zero-shot strategy employs a cascading hierarchical structure. The initial inference pass utilizes broad, overarching prompts such as "motorized vehicle situated on an impervious surface." Once the macro-level instances are aggregated to consolidate sparse object predictions, secondary conditional prompts are applied exclusively within those masked boundaries to perform fine-grained classification. This sequential reduction of the semantic search space prevents the model from misallocating attention across visually similar but taxonomically distinct targets, drastically reducing the false positive rate.

### **Spatial Descriptors and Viewpoint Context Encoding**

Vision-Language Models exhibit an overwhelming bias toward ground-level photography. When queried with the word "building," the text encoder disproportionately activates latent features associated with facades, windows, and doors—features that are entirely occluded in a top-down orthophoto8. Consequently, prompt engineering in the 2024–2025 literature dictates the mandatory inclusion of explicit spatial and viewpoint descriptors.  
By systematically prepending viewpoint modifiers such as "nadir aerial view of," "satellite imagery depicting," or "overhead top-down perspective of," the text embedding is forcefully shifted into the geospatial domain of the shared latent space. This linguistic framing instructs the cross-attention mechanisms within SAM 3 to prioritize the geometric footprints, roof topologies, and cast shadows of the objects, rather than searching for lateral visual cues that do not exist in the Darmstadt DOP20 dataset8.

### **Exploiting the Presence Head for Negative Prompting**

A defining architectural innovation of SAM 3 is the introduction of the presence head, which estimates the probability that a queried concept actually exists within the image, mathematically decoupling the recognition aspect ("what") from the localization aspect ("where")3. The final confidence score for a mask is calculated as the product of the presence probability and the localization score3.  
This decoupling presents a powerful avenue for zero-shot false positive suppression through negative prompting. In remote sensing, vast tracts of imagery consist of complex backgrounds that trigger spurious localizations when evaluated at the patch level1. By explicitly prompting the model with visually similar negative classes—for instance, prompting for "dark asphalt pavement" and "dense forest canopy shadow" when the true target is "water bodies"—the presence head is forced to evaluate and assign probabilities to the background confounders. The SegEarth-OV-3 pipeline can utilize these presence scores dynamically; if the presence probability of the negative prompt exceeds that of the target prompt for a given region, the localization mask is discarded. This presence-guided filtering drastically reduces the incidence of fragmented, overlapping masks in dense urban scenes without requiring any post-processing threshold adjustments1.

## **Resolution-Aware Prompting and Domain Adaptation**

The domain gap between a 5cm GSD and a 20cm GSD is not merely a difference in scale, but a fundamental alteration in available information. At 5cm, the SegEarth-OV-3 backbone operates on rich, high-frequency textural details. At 20cm, these details are irreversibly lost, and the model must rely almost exclusively on low-frequency structural contours and broader contextual relationships4. The challenge lies in adapting the model to this loss of information without retraining the feature extractors.

### **Linguistic Encoding of Resolution Context**

Because large vision-language models have been exposed to varying qualities of imagery during their web-scale pre-training, their latent spaces inherently encode concepts of resolution and blur5. It is entirely feasible to utilize text prompts as a zero-shot domain adaptation mechanism by explicitly declaring the resolution constraints within the query.  
Modifying a standard prompt to include phrases like "low-resolution aerial view of" or "blurred, low-contrast pixel cluster representing a" alters the semantic expectation of the object's appearance. By anticipating a degraded visual token, the text embedding aligns more favorably with the feature representations produced by the frozen PE-L+ backbone when processing the 20cm Darmstadt data5. This linguistic acknowledgment of the resolution gap prevents the model from penalizing predictions that lack the sharp internal textures expected from high-resolution imagery.

### **Prompt-Based Domain Adaptation via Region-Adaptive Magnification**

When the physical resolution of the target domain is fundamentally incompatible with the receptive field expectations of the foundation model, prompt engineering alone is insufficient. The SOPSeg (Small Object Prompt Segmentation) framework identifies that SAM's performance degrades catastrophically on small objects because the coarse 1/16 feature resolution of the ViT encoder causes a severe loss of fine spatial details6.  
To bridge this scale gap, one can implement a region-adaptive magnification strategy prior to inference. This involves artificially upsampling the 20cm Darmstadt imagery patches to simulate the geometric scale of the 5cm Potsdam imagery. While standard algorithms like bicubic interpolation cannot invent missing high-frequency textures, expanding the spatial footprint of the objects allows SAM 3's windowed and global attention mechanisms to properly engage with the structural boundaries6.  
In a gradient-free pipeline, this upsampling serves as a crucial data-space domain adapter. The upscaled image patches are fed into the frozen backbone, allowing the existing attention heads to operate on pixel groupings that match their pre-trained geometric expectations. Following the generation of the segmentation logits, the masks are subsequently downscaled to map back to the original 20cm coordinate space. This strategy dramatically improves the recall of small objects such as vehicles and isolated vegetation patches that would otherwise be entirely overlooked by the network's minimum resolution thresholds6.  
Furthermore, the concept of adaptive scale-aware feature extraction, as seen in networks like UASNet, suggests that manipulating the spatial orientation of the prompts can further aid the model. Providing oriented bounding box prompts, rather than standard horizontal boxes, aligns the prompt geometry directly with the structural layout of the aerial objects, giving the frozen decoder much tighter spatial constraints to guide the generation of accurate mask boundaries despite the low resolution6.

## **Gradient-Free Test-Time Adaptation and Inference Strategies**

Test-Time Adaptation (TTA) traditionally refers to the process of updating model weights on the fly using unsupervised losses derived from incoming target data. However, the strict constraint to avoid any gradient updates on the SAM 3 backbone necessitates a reliance on feature aggregation, normalization recalibration, and inference-time structural regularization.

### **Context-Aware Inference (ConInfer)**

One of the most potent training-free mechanisms for handling remote sensing imagery is the Context-Aware Inference (ConInfer) framework13. Standard open-vocabulary pipelines evaluate imagery in isolated patches, which is fundamentally misaligned with the nature of Earth Observation data. Remote sensing scenes exhibit massive scale and profound spatial and semantic correlations—a road in one patch is highly likely to continue into the next13. Isolated patch-wise prediction results in severe segmentation inconsistencies and fragmented class assignments14.  
ConInfer addresses this by performing joint predictions across multiple spatial units while explicitly modeling their inter-unit dependencies, entirely without gradients. It achieves this by bridging the semantic alignment capabilities of the VLM with the structural reasoning capabilities of an auxiliary, frozen vision foundation model, typically DINOv313. DINOv3 is renowned for its ability to generate highly coherent, dense structural features that excel at local geometry and part-level correspondence, even without semantic labels.  
During inference on the Darmstadt dataset, the imagery is simultaneously processed by the SegEarth-OV-3 pipeline to generate semantic probabilities, and by DINOv3 to extract high-level contextual features. ConInfer then uses the DINO-derived features to spatially group visually coherent regions, enforcing a structural regularization upon the VLM's outputs. If the VLM produces noisy, high-entropy predictions along a 20cm blurred boundary, the DINO features act as a spatial smoothing constraint, forcing visually continuous regions to share the same semantic label13. This integration of global contextual cues significantly enhances segmentation robustness and consistency, achieving average improvements of over 6% in open-vocabulary object extraction without a single weight update13.

### **Confidence-Weighted Gaussian Ensembling for Overlapping Crops**

Because remote sensing datasets vastly exceed the input resolution limits of ViT encoders, sliding window inference is mandatory. A standard approach of extracting contiguous patches, predicting masks, and stitching them together creates severe edge artifacts, as the receptive field of the network is artificially truncated at the patch boundaries11. Simple averaging of overlapping patches only partially mitigates this issue and often results in blurred segmentation contours.  
Optimal inference strategies deploy a 2D Gaussian kernel weighting matrix during the aggregation of overlapping crops11. As the sliding window traverses the image with a defined stride (e.g., a 50% overlap), the raw logits produced by SAM 3 are multiplied by a Gaussian distribution centered on the patch. This ensures that pixels located near the center of the crop, which benefit from a complete spatial context and maximum receptive field coverage, are assigned a confidence weight approaching 1.0. Conversely, pixels near the borders of the crop are heavily discounted with weights approaching 0.012. When the overlapping crops are fused to reconstruct the massive aerial tile, the integral prediction is overwhelmingly determined by the high-confidence central inferences, seamlessly smoothing away boundary discontinuities and maintaining crisp segmentation edges even across the 20cm resolution data11.  
This can be further augmented by incorporating a truncated-Gaussian-weighted reliability metric, as proposed in the TGR-T framework for weakly supervised segmentation27. By tracking the confidence statistics (mean and variance) of the predictions across the overlapping batches, the system can establish a dynamic threshold. Predictions that fall below this threshold—indicating severe visual ambiguity—are softly down-weighted, allowing the more confident overlapping inferences to dictate the final pixel assignment27.

### **Adaptive Batch Normalization (AdaBN)**

While the attention matrices and projection weights of SAM 3 remain strictly frozen, the internal normalization statistics of the network can be adapted to the target domain seamlessly. Adaptive Batch Normalization (AdaBN) is a highly effective, gradient-free TTA strategy that addresses domain shifts resulting from changes in sensor characteristics, lighting, and spatial resolution16.  
When SegEarth-OV-3 processes the 5cm Potsdam data, the normalization layers within the backbone calculate and store specific running means and variances. When applied to the 20cm Darmstadt data, these stored statistics are no longer representative of the input feature distribution, causing the network's activations to shift into suboptimal non-linear regimes. AdaBN circumvents this by passing a set of unlabelled target images through the network in a purely forward-pass calibration phase16. The running mean and variance statistics in the normalization layers are allowed to update based on the Darmstadt data, while all actual network weights remain frozen. This autonomous recalibration requires negligible computational overhead and shifts the internal feature representations to align with the lower contrast and softer edges of the 20cm imagery, resulting in stable model generalization across diverse geographic scenarios16.

### **Visual-Guided Prompt Bias via Entropy Minimization**

The Test-Time Multi-Prompt Adaptation (TMPA) framework introduces a quasi-gradient-free mechanism where the visual backbone is untouched, but the text embeddings are dynamically adapted during inference based on the visual features of the test image15.  
When evaluating regions of an image where the textual prompt and the visual reality are misaligned (due to the 20cm resolution degradation), the model naturally outputs predictions with high entropy, indicating profound uncertainty. The Visual-Guided Test-Time Adaptation (VGTA) strategy within TMPA identifies highly confident visual features (low entropy predictions) from the current target image and uses them to construct a textual "prompt bias"15.  
An optimization loop is executed exclusively on the lightweight prompt embedding vector. By performing gradient descent on the prompt to minimize a pixel-level entropy loss against the high-confidence visual features, the text embedding is mathematically dragged toward the visual reality of the specific scene being analyzed15. This guarantees that the language prompt perfectly matches the specific textural and geographic conditions of the Darmstadt tile without requiring any backpropagation through the PE-L+ backbone, effectively closing the domain gap in the shared latent space15.

## **Minimum Viable Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) for Extreme Low-Data Regimes**

If the strict gradient-free constraint is marginally relaxed to permit Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT), the primary challenge becomes adapting the model using a severely restricted support set consisting of exactly 5 labeled images. Fine-tuning a massive foundation model on a 5-shot dataset introduces a critical risk of neural collapse and catastrophic forgetting. The network is highly prone to memorizing the specific lighting, seasonal conditions, and idiosyncratic geometries of those 5 tiles, destroying the expansive zero-shot representations acquired during pre-training10.  
To adapt SegEarth-OV-3 under these extreme low-data constraints, the intervention must utilize the absolute minimum number of trainable parameters while employing aggressive regularization to maintain generalizability across the broader Darmstadt dataset.

### **Optimal PEFT Architectures: PTSAM and CAT-SAM**

Standard LoRA implementations that inject low-rank matrices across all attention layers of the ViT encoder are excessively risky in a 5-shot scenario, as they still expose too many parameters to the limited data distribution29.  
**Decoder-Only Prompt Tuning (PTSAM):** The most conservative and highly effective approach in recent literature is the Prompt-Tuned SAM (PTSAM) methodology. Research demonstrates that transforming SAM from a generalist to a specialized domain expert requires as few as 2,048 additional trainable parameters10. By maintaining a strictly frozen heavyweight image encoder and prompt encoder, PTSAM introduces lightweight soft prompt tokens exclusively into the cross-attention layers of the mask decoder10. This ensures that the core visual feature extraction remains untouched, preventing catastrophic forgetting of foundational object geometries. The 5-shot training solely teaches the mask decoder how to interpret the blurry, low-resolution 20cm features to delineate boundaries that match the target domain expectations, achieving performance on par with heavy fine-tuning methods while requiring roughly 2,000× fewer trainable parameters10.  
**ConditionAl Tuning (CAT-SAM):** When the domain shift is severe enough that decoder-only tuning is insufficient, CAT-SAM (ConditionAl Tuning) offers a balanced alternative. Recognizing the massive parameter imbalance between the heavyweight encoder and the lightweight decoder, CAT-SAM establishes a specific "prompt bridge" that maps domain-specific features generated in the mask decoder back into the image encoder9. This enables a synergistic, decoder-conditioned joint tuning. The network captures the necessary resolution-specific feature adaptations while strictly limiting the magnitude of the encoder updates, ensuring data-efficient adaptation from the 5-shot target samples9.  
**Convolutional LoRA (Conv-LoRA):** Because the SAM 3 backbone utilizes a plain Vision Transformer, it lacks the inductive biases—such as translation invariance and local multi-scale priors—that make Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) inherently robust to resolution changes28. When fine-tuning on aerial imagery, the Conv-LoRA architecture integrates ultra-lightweight convolution operations directly within the bottleneck structure of the LoRA matrices28. During the 5-shot adaptation, these convolutional biases force the ViT to relearn the capacity to extract high-level semantic features from the local spatial relationships of the blurred 20cm imagery, significantly outperforming standard Visual Prompt Tuning (VPT) on complex multi-class semantic segmentation tasks28.

### **Mitigating Overfitting in the 5-Shot Regime**

To prevent the PEFT modules from completely overfitting to the 5 labeled Darmstadt tiles, advanced regularization and data augmentation techniques are required.  
**Diffusion-Based Resolution Bridge Augmentation:** Traditional geometric augmentations (rotations, flips) are insufficient to prevent memorization in a 5-shot scenario. Recent frameworks propose utilizing Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) framed as an image inpainting task to artificially expand the support set21. An off-the-shelf diffusion model, conditioned on the 5 labeled tiles, synthesizes high-fidelity, plausible variations of the scenes21. By introducing diverse synthetic lighting, atmospheric conditions, and background textures while explicitly preserving the structural segmentation masks, the diffusion model constructs a "resolution bridge." This exponentially increases the sample variance presented to the PEFT modules, forcing them to learn robust, generalized representations of the 20cm features rather than memorizing the idiosyncratic noise of the original 5 images22.  
**Geometry-Aware Regularization (RegionSmoothLoss):** The SAM-Aug framework introduces a powerful regularization concept utilizing the zero-shot priors of the foundation model itself. Before any fine-tuning occurs, the frozen SAM 3 model generates baseline masks for the 5-shot dataset32. While these zero-shot masks may be semantically inaccurate, their geometric contours are generally robust. During the PEFT optimization, a RegionSmoothLoss is appended to the objective function. This consistency regularization term penalizes the network if the updated predictions exhibit high variance within the continuous geometric regions established by the zero-shot priors32. This forces the PEFT updates to produce stable, semantically smooth predictions, effectively neutralizing the noise and boundary fragmentation that typically characterize models overfitted to minimal data32.  
**Adaptive Rank Selection (ARENA):** Standard LoRA applications deploy a fixed rank (![][image1]) across all adapted layers, which is highly inefficient and heightens the risk of overfitting in extreme few-shot regimes29. The Adaptive Rank Segmentation (ARENA) methodology addresses this by introducing an ![][image2] sparsity regularizer to the fine-tuning loss function, treated via a proximal optimizer29. This regularizer acts as a direct penalty on the singular value decomposition rank of the trainable weight matrices. As the 5-shot fine-tuning progresses, ARENA automatically prunes unnecessary rank capacities, forcing the network to discover and utilize only the absolute minimum task-adapted rank required to parse the 20cm Darmstadt imagery29. This mathematical constraint provides a rigorous defense against overfitting while maximizing parameter efficiency.

## **Synthesizing SegEarth-OV and PEFT for Remote Sensing Domain Adaptation**

The trajectory of the 2024–2026 literature demonstrates a clear convergence between inherently training-free Open-Vocabulary Remote Sensing methods (such as SegEarth-OV) and Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning targeted at closing severe domain gaps.  
The foundational SegEarth-OV framework pioneered the use of external units, specifically the SimFeatUp upsampler, which acts as a universal feature restorer for low-resolution inputs, correcting distorted target shapes and ill-fitting boundaries without task-specific post-training26. While SegEarth-OV-3 champions a fully frozen approach, subsequent architectures prove that coupling SAM-based open-vocabulary models with lightweight PEFT yields unprecedented cross-domain resilience.  
The Domain Prompt Learning with Quaternion Networks (DPLQ) framework, for instance, tackles the precise challenge of adapting large-scale VLMs to specialized domains34. By placing visual features on the imaginary axis and language prompts on the real axis within a quaternion space, DPLQ models cross-modal relationships orthogonally. This mathematical separation, combined with the injection of scaled Gaussian noise into the quaternion space, facilitates an incredibly robust adaptation from generalized natural images to specific remote sensing features, effectively bridging gaps like the 5cm to 20cm resolution shift while avoiding overfitting34.  
Similarly, the RS2-SAM2 framework introduces a bidirectional hierarchical fusion module that embeds prompt tuning directly within and after the frozen SAM image encoder35. This architecture specifically targets the interpretation of text-described remote sensing scenes, utilizing a mask prompt generator to translate multimodal features into dense pseudo-mask prompts, providing SAM with the highly precise spatial cues necessary to navigate blurred or ambiguous boundaries35.  
Finally, the SAMOIS framework directly addresses the decline in SAM's effectiveness on non-natural domains by integrating LoRA and adapter techniques specifically to capture multi-scale features in remote sensing imagery36. By establishing this lightweight fine-tuning strategy on the encoder, SAMOIS explicitly targets the lack of accurate category labels and the presence of complex backgrounds, preserving the core architecture while optimizing for the unique characteristics of Earth Observation data36.

## **Strategic Conclusions**

Deploying SegEarth-OV-3 to the 20cm GSD Darmstadt DOP20 imagery after optimization on the 5cm GSD Potsdam dataset demands interventions that respect the constraints of the frozen foundation model.  
The most immediate, highest-yield strategies involve zero-shot prompt engineering and gradient-free test-time adaptations. Generating diverse, context-aware descriptions via LLMs, utilizing hierarchical prompting, and actively deploying negative prompts through the presence head will significantly reduce the semantic confusion that cripples open-vocabulary performance on aerial data. Furthermore, bridging the resolution gap can be effectively managed without retraining by employing region-adaptive magnification to simulate higher-resolution spatial footprints, utilizing Gaussian-weighted sliding window aggregation to eliminate edge artifacts, and deploying Context-Aware Inference (ConInfer) to enforce spatial and semantic coherence via frozen DINOv3 structural priors.  
If the protocol allows for minimal parameter updates bounded by a 5-shot limit, the integration of decoder-only prompt tuning (PTSAM) or Conv-LoRA offers a mathematically sound pathway to domain specialization. These PEFT methods must be strictly regulated using Adaptive Rank Selection (ARENA) and supported by Diffusion-based synthetic data expansion to proactively neutralize the severe risks of catastrophic forgetting and neural collapse. By systematically applying these resolution-aware and mathematically constrained methodologies, the SegEarth-OV-3 pipeline can achieve robust, high-fidelity segmentation across profound domain shifts while preserving the foundational generalization capabilities of the SAM 3 architecture.
